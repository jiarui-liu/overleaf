# ACL 2026 — Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics

## Special Theme
ACL 2026 theme: **"Explainability of NLP Models"** — especially welcomes work on evaluating explanation quality, detecting bias through explanations, and designing inherently interpretable architectures.

## Page Limits
- **Long papers:** 8 pages of content (9 pages camera-ready)
- **Short papers:** 4 pages of content (5 pages camera-ready)
- **References:** Unlimited, do not count toward limit
- **Limitations section:** Required, does NOT count toward page limit
- **Ethical Considerations section:** Optional, does NOT count toward page limit

## Required Sections
- Abstract (10 pt, **maximum 200 words**)
- Introduction
- Related Work
- Methodology / Approach
- Experiments / Results
- Conclusion
- **Limitations (REQUIRED)** — placed after conclusion
- **Ethical Considerations** (recommended)
- References

## Formatting
- Official **ACL style files** (LaTeX or Word)
- A4 paper (21 cm × 29.7 cm), two-column format
- Body text: 11 pt Times Roman, single-spaced
- Section titles: 12 pt bold; Paper title: 15 pt bold
- Abstract: 10 pt, max 200 words
- References and captions: 10 pt
- **Review versions must include line numbers**
- **Double-blind:** no author names, no identifying self-references

## Reviewer Evaluation Criteria (Three Scores)

1. **Soundness** — Technical correctness and validity. Is the methodology sound? Does the evidence support the claims? Are experiments well-designed and reproducible?

2. **Excitement** — Impact and interest to the NLP community. How novel and interesting is the contribution? Papers can score high even with modest technical novelty if they open new directions.

3. **Overall Recommendation** — Balances soundness, excitement, novelty, and potential impact.

## Key Principles
- Papers need NOT achieve state-of-the-art to be accepted
- Reviewers recognize diversity in methodology — papers need not use deep learning, focus on English, or use traditional experimental designs
- Claims must be proportional to evidence provided

## Common Writing-Related Rejection Reasons
- Missing Limitations section (required)
- Abstract exceeds 200 words or is uninformative
- Claims not proportional to evidence
- Missing comparison to relevant baselines
- Line numbers missing in review version
- No discussion of potential negative societal impacts
- Formatting violations (wrong template, wrong paper size)
